PROPOSAL SUMMARY

The Neogene is a period when the Earth underwent through drastic modifications in the processes that ultimately control the evolution of sedimentary systems: climate and tectonics. It is in the Neogene that Earth’s continental plates acquired its modern shape, modifying sea level and patterns of oceanic currents that intensified ongoing climatic changes. At the same time, a pulse in tectonic activity generated new orogenic areas, increasing the availability of source rocks. Sedimentary systems, as a secondary process in Earth’s system, respond promptly to the reorganization of the primary forces, and hence, the signals of the Neogene events are recorded in the stratigraphic record of continental margins.
Particularly, influxes of siliciclastic sediment in global continental margins have been observed in the middle-to-late Miocene period (Lavier 2001, Steckler 1999, Sanchez 2012, Tcherepanov 2010, Lu 2004). An increase in orogeny, and an abrupt change in global δ18O(‰) isotope values after the mid-Miocene climatic optimum peak are also documented for this 10 Ma interval (Potter et al., 2009; Potter et al., 2015). Cooler temperatures are hypothesized to have led to a decrease in precipitation rates, reducing vegetation cover in sediment source areas and resulting in more efficient erosion and transport of sediment by fluvial systems.
IODP Expeditions 356 - Indonesian Throughflow and 369 - Australia Cretaceous Climate and Tectonics are recovering Neogene strata in the Western Continental Margin of Australia, representing a latitudinal transect of cores from 17S to 35S, fully comprehending the Western Australian Margin. This research proposal is developed within the major objectives of Expeditions 356 and 369, and intends to analyze the stratigraphic evolution of continental margins during the Neogene, its interactions and feedbacks with climatic and oceanographic events. Specifically, we focus on siliciclastic deposition during the middle to late Miocene in Western Australia continental margin. The project is split depending on the focus area, and the main methodology used (seismic stratigraphy; core and log correlation; numerical modeling). A three-step research approach is proposed: (1) middle-late Miocene siliciclastic influx on the Australian Northwest Shelf - origins and potential links to global events; (2) Expedition 356 and 369 transect - Neogene paleoclimate variation along Western Australia; (3) a coupled stratigraphic - landscape evolution model of SW Australian continental margin - climate-tectonic interactions in the Neogene.
The project will test several individual hypotheses (see proposal in detail), clarifying local and regional problems, but ultimately, we intend to contribute to two main scientific problems: (1) is there a global event of siliciclastic deposition in continental margins during the middle to late Miocene; (2) what are the local and global mechanisms that controlled siliciclastic deposition in continental margins during the Miocene.

INTRODUCTION

Continental margins represent a rich record of Earth history, the result and consequence of a myriad of processes and interactions that impact the continent-ocean intersection through geological time (e.g., fluvial and marine processes, biotic and more recently, human activity). According to the United Nations, 44% of the human population lives within 150 km of coastal areas, and are impacted by natural processes that occur at continental margins. Stratigraphic studies of the long-term Cenozoic evolution of continental margins can shed light on how future climate change will modify these environments. In terms of energy exploration, 17% of world’s petroleum reserves are located in middle and upper Miocene deposits that represent only 2% of the Phanerozoic \citep{Potter_2009}.
The events and processes that have affected these areas in the past can be comprehended by the detailed examination of continental margin stratigraphy and geomorphology, both to elucidate past landscape evolution and predict that of the future. For example, the climatic and tectonic activity can be deciphered by identifying the signals left by these events in the strata preserved in continental margins. However, in order to understand the origins of the stratigraphic evolution of continental margins, it is essential to decipher the relative roles of local/regional causes vs. global causes. Although global processes (e.g., climate, eustatic changes) are directly linked to stratigraphic and basin evolution, modern research on continental margins is revealing that local conditions may overcome global signals, obscuring the impact of global events. Therefore, stratigraphic correlation between widely separated continental margins is not straightforward, since local conditions (e.g., subsidence pattern and sediment supply) influence stratigraphic architectures.
In that context, the Neogene period is marked by dramatic changes in climate and tectonic patterns (e.g., Tibetan Plateau uplift, development of the Antarctic ice sheet), that affected sea level and sediment supply rates and, consequently, continental margin evolution. A general trend of increasing terrestrial sedimentation rates is observed throughout the Cenozoic on many different margins \citep{Molnar_2004}. Particularly, in the Neogene, an increase in siliciclastic supply has been linked to the establishment of a climatic cooling trend that began during the middle Miocene and intensified on the Pliocene and Quaternary \citep{Zachos_2001}. Such cooling is hypothesized to have led to a decrease in precipitation rates, reducing vegetation cover in sediment source areas and resulting in more efficient erosion and transport of sediment by fluvial systems. An increase in aridity, therefore, resulted in increased sedimentation supply to margins. However, although this relationship has been observed in modern analogs and numerical models, a solid link between climatic events and sediment supply in the stratigraphic record of the Neogene has yet to be established.
Potter and Szatmari \citep{Potter_2009,Potter_2015} suggest that both the increase in sedimentation rates and the alteration in climate patterns that developed during the Miocene are the result of global plate tectonic processes. Based on a qualitative review of tectonic events during the Miocene, the authors argue that the movement of the Antarctic continent towards the south pole, the establishment of six oceanic gateways, and anomalous activity in two mantle “superplumes” below the Pacific and African plates, led to an increase in ice sheet development, a reorganization in the global oceanic currents, and a peak in tectonic orogeny elsewhere, including in areas remote from the superplumes. The reorganization of oceanic currents led to the development of the Antarctic circumpolar current, which formed a barrier between cold Antarctic waters and warmer equatorial waters. This event modified climate globally and established a cooling trend after the mid-Miocene climatic optimum \citep{Zachos_2001}.
Subsequently, ice-sheet development and an increase in aridity resulted in lower sea level, reduced vegetation cover, and increasing in erosion rates. Cooler temperatures also amplified the effects of normally weak Milankovitch cycles, increasing amplitudes and frequencies of continental ice-sheet expansion and contraction and of associated eustatic oscillations \citep{Eyles_1996}. At the same time, new sedimentary source areas were formed by orogeny during the mid-Miocene also contributed to the increased rates of sediment supply rate to margins. These patterns are observed in the stratigraphic records on continental margins worldwide, with the development of progradational sets \citep{Bartek_1991}, the shutdown of carbonate factories, development of karst features, the growth of incised fluvial channels, and dominance of high-frequency Milankovitch cycles. All of these sedimentary features represent the stratigraphic response to sea-level fall, with increasing sedimentation rates, and global cooling \cite{Cathro_2003,Liu_2011,Sanchez_2012}.
The Middle-to-Late Miocene, after the mid-Miocene climatic optimum, is a period of increasing sediment supply and global cooling. Influxes of siliciclastic sediment during this period have been observed on the New Jersey margin (Steckler 1999), in the Gulf of Mexico \cite{Galloway_2008,Galloway_2011}, in the Mediterranean \citep{John_2003}, on the West African margin (Lavier 2001), Australian margin (Sanchez 2012, Tcherepanov 2010\cite{Cathro_2003,Liu_2011,Sanchez_2012}, and off New Zealand \citep{Lu_2004}. The case of the Australian Northwest Shelf (Northern Carnarvon Basin) is particularly interesting, since the middle-to-late Miocene sandstones of the Bare Formation constitute a unique episode of siliciclastic sedimentation on a carbonate-dominated margin. The abrupt change in global δ18O (‰) isotope values after the mid-Miocene climatic optimum peak coupled with increased orogeny make the mid-to-late Miocene an important focal point for constraining global vs. local effects on the stratigraphic record.
IODP Expedition 356 Indonesian Throughflow drilled and cored seven new sites of the Neogene of the Australia Northwest Shelf in 2015, describing for the first time, in high resolution, the Neogene strata in the margin. Additionally, IODP Expedition 369 Australia Cretaceous Climate and Tectonics will be drilling six sites in the Western Margin and one site in the Southern Margin of Australia. Scientific objectives of these expeditions for the Miocene include: constrain effects of the climatic transition for the sedimentary evolution of the margin, and how it varies latitudinally (e.g. transition from cool-water carbonates to tropical carbonates); the onset of the Leeuwin current; onset of the Indonesian Throughflow current; effects of the Tasman passages’ opening and restriction of the Indonesian Gateway in the sedimentary evolution of the margin. Jointly, these research efforts represent an opportunity to investigate the variability of Neogene deposition along the Western Australian margins, its variation in siliciclastic content, and its adaptation to latitudinal changes, from 17S to 35S (i.e. climate effects). A coauthor of this study participated in Expedition 356 and the author will be participating in Expedition 369 in September of 2016.
This research proposal aims to analyze the stratigraphic evolution of continental margins during the Neogene, its interactions and feedbacks with climatic and oceanographic events. Specifically, we focus on siliciclastic deposition during the middle to late Miocene in Western Australia continental margin. There are numerous problems that needed to be addressed, including: Do we observe a global or local increase in the siliciclastic deposition in continental margins during the middle Miocene? How do these different formations correlate in age and extension? Are local conditions deviating the characteristics of deposits from a possibly global pattern? In this proposal, three phases of the project are discretized and explained: (1) middle-late Miocene siliciclastic influx on the Australian Northwest Shelf - origins and potential links to global events; (2) Expedition 356 and 369 transect - Neogene paleoclimate variation along Western Australia; (3) a coupled stratigraphic - landscape evolution model of SW Australian continental margin - climate-tectonic interactions in the Neogene.

AREA OF STUDY

Western Continental Margin of Australia

The study area comprises the Continental margin of Western Australia and can be divided into two main blocks: the Australian Northwest Shelf (NWS; and the Australian Southwest Shelf (SWS).

Northwest Shelf and Northern Carnarvon Basin

Formed as a rifted margin in the late Permian, during the initial breakup of eastern Gondwana  (CITE). Two subsequent phases of tectonic extension shaped the modern character of the margin: in the Late Triassic–Early Jurassic; and in the Late Jurassic \citep{Driscoll_1998}, that culminated with the separation of India and Australia in the early Cretaceous (Heine and Müller, 2005). In the Late Miocene, the formation of the Timor-Banda Arc subduction zone in northern NWS resulted in plate reorganization, with inversion of extensional faults \citep{Cathro_2003}. Oceanographically, NWS is affected by the South Equatorial Current in the north, and by the shallow Leeuwin Current (Gallagher et al., 2017). The onset of Leeuwin is not fully constrained, with estimates ranging from Late Eocene (McGowran et al., 1997) to 500 ka (Kendrick et al., 1991). Modern studies, however, are converging to a Late Miocene-Pliocene age of initiation of the Leeuwin current (Sinha et al., 2006; Karas et al., 2011).
Mesozoic northward movement of the Australian tectonic plate into tropical latitudes is synchronous with a transition from siliciclastic to carbonate deposition on the margin (Veevers et al., 1991; Müller et al., 2008a). Carbonate deposition from the Eocene to late Miocene comprise calcilutites on the slope, calcisiltites and calcarenites near clinoform rollovers, and calcarenites on paleoshelves (Hull and Griffiths, 2002; Moss et al., 2004). Reefs or reef mounds occur in the Oligocene–Miocene section (Romine et al., 1997; Cathro et al., 2003; Ryan et al., 2009;\citep{Liu_2011}. Cenozoic sedimentation is continuous along the NWS (Apthorpe, 1988), not restricted by Mesozoic basins and sub-basins.

Southwest Margin and Mentelle Basin

The southwest margin of Australia consists of the Perth and Mentelle basins, as well as the Wallaby and Naturaliste plateaus (Bradshaw et al., 2003; Norvick, 2004). The Mentelle Basin is divided into eastern and western sub-basins (Bradshaw et al., 2003; Borissova et al., 2010), and is separated to the Perth basin by the structural high of the Yallingup Shelf. Paleozoic to Mesozoic development of SWS was controlled by multiple rifting phases in early Cretaceous that resulted in the break-up of eastern Gondwana (Bradshaw et al., 2003; Norvick, 2004). Miocene tectonic inversion is also observed across the SWS, with minor volcanic activity in the Houtman sub-basin (Daniell et al., 2010; Rollet et al., 2013a). DSDP 258 drilled the only stratigraphic well in the Mentelle Basin, containing the upper part of the post-rift succession to mid-Albian interval (Davies et al, 1974). Cenozoic strata of the Mentelle Basin are not understood. The condensed and discontinuous character of the section makes it difficult to differentiate and correlate (Bradshaw et al., 2003). DSDP 264, observed three Cenozoic units separated by unconformities: a middle Paleocene-lower Eocene chalk; an upper Eocene ooze; and an upper Miocene-Holocene ooze (Davies et al, 1974).
Data area is located in the Mentelle Basin and Naturaliste Plateau, in the southernmost portion of SWS (Figure 1). Targeted sites will allow comparison of deep water (site 8A) and intermediate water (site 9A) locations. Drilling is expected to recover continuous sequences of Paleocene–Eocene and Upper Miocene–Recent sequences strata. Particularly, sites MBAS-6A, MBAS-8A, and MBAS-9A are planned to recover Neogene sediments.

SCIENTIFIC OBJECTIVE

I. Middle-late Miocene siliciclastic influx on the Australian Northwest Shelf: origins and potential links to global events

The Bare Formation in the Northern Carnarvon Basin represents a unique episode of siliciclastic deposition within a carbonate-dominated system in the middle to late Miocene of the NWS, a period when anomalous tectonic and climatic activity led to an abrupt increase in continental-margin sedimentation worldwide. The Bare Fm. has been previously identified in the Dampier sub-basin, Beagle sub-basin, and Rankin platform, in the southeastern portion of NCB \citep{Sanchez_2012a}. Recent drilling by IODP Expedition 356 recovered a partially continuous record of Neogene carbonate deposition (packstones, wackestone, and grainstones), in an overall shallow environment that is only interrupted by the presence of siliciclastic deposition of the Bare Fm. during the Late Miocene (Figure 2). Presence anhydrite coeval with siliciclastic deposition indicates it was deposited during a period of regional aridity and low relative sea level. Site U1462 represents the most detailed description of the carbonate and siliciclastic deposits of the Neogene of the NCB, that drilled and cored a depth below seafloor (CSF) of 946.09 m. Lithologically, site U1462C described medium to coarse quartz-sandstone, interbedded with calcarenite and dolomite.
In light of new information and interpretations developed by IODP 356 research team, our objective is to reinterpreted the stratigraphy, the depositional system, and timing of deposition of the Bare Fm., in the context of regional climatic and tectonics events of NWS.

Data

Data area is located at the Dampier sub-basin, where the thickest package of the Bare Fm. develops (Figure 1). Lithostratigraphy of Site U1462 is divided into four units (Figure 5). Lithologic descriptions are based on sediments recovered from Holes U1462A (0–849.6 m core depth below seafloor [CSF-A]), U1462B (0–47.8 m CSF-A), and U1462C (33–946.09 m CSF-A). The holes were aligned based on drilling depth and correlation of lithologic boundaries (Gallagher et al., 2017). Nannofossil analyses were done at 10 m resolution and additional benthic foraminiferal samples were taken from selected intervals, to define paleobathymetry and biostratigraphic age (Gallagher et al., 2017). In this work, we coupled the new lithological descriptions and biostratigraphic dating provided by IODP Exp 356 with 2D and 3D seismic stratigraphy analysis. Seismic data include a set of 3D MCS surveys, with a total area of 8000 km2, and a dense 2D seismic data grid with a total area of 29460 km2. Central frequencies for the MCS data range from 32 to 50 Hz, with a vertical resolution of around 15 m in the study interval (velocity of 2000 m/s). Seismic-well tie is performed using FMS-sonic and VSI (versatile seismic image) p-wave velocity logs from U1462C. 3D and 2D MCS data were donated to the University of Texas Institute for Geophysics (UTIG) by Woodside Petroleum, Ltd. and Geoscience Australia.

Hypothesis

  • Depositional System of the Bare Fm: Deltaic vs. Beach-barrier environment.
Using seismic interpretation, \citep{Sanchez_2012,Sanchez_2012a} identified 27 delta lobes and suggested that siliciclastic deposition of the Bare Fm. occurred as a deltaic progradation over the carbonate-dominated shelf during sea-level fall in the Late Miocene. A deltaic interpretation for the Bare Fm. implies a major change of climate conditions in the hinterland in a short period of time, that affected run-off activity and delta progradation. Alternatively, Smith et al. (2014) O’Callaghan et al. (2014) and Griben et al. (2016), argued that the absence of recognizable fluvial channels in 3D seismic data, the along-strike orientation of the deposits, and the interpretation of regional geology advocate for a beach-barrier depositional system. Our analysis intends to untie the present duality in the interpretation of the depositional system. 3D Seismic stratigraphy and coherence attribute analysis is performed in order to identify singular features that may allow us to differentiate between the two interpretations. For example, at the resolution of the seismic survey, we expect to observe fluvial channels, channel belts, and deltaic forms, what would support a deltaic interpretation. Considering the large volume of siliciclastic sedimentation, fluvial channel incisions, and channel filling, should also be observed. On the other hand, apresence of lateral accretion and beachlines, with a predominantly along-strike morphology for the deposits would favors a beach-barrier interpretation.
  • The Bare Fm. represents a stratigraphic response to environmental changes in the climate and oceanographic system of the NWS.
Siliciclastic deposition over the NWS carbonate margin has been previously connected to climatic transition in continental Australia (Groneveld et al., 2017; Christensen et al., 2017). Specifically, variability of quartz content (%) throughout the Cenozoic may reflects transition between arid and wet periods in NW Australia regional climate cells. The connection is that an increase in humidity in a previously arid environment intensifies runoff processes, and subsequently, fluvial transport of siliciclastic sediments to the margins. On the other hand, sedimentary processes on the coast are also affected by longshore processes and wave action. Volume of sediment reworking in the shelf could be either strengthened or weakened, depending on the intensity of coastal processes. Neogene oceanographic events on the NWS include the constraining of the Indonesian gateway and the establishment of the deep and cold Indonesian Throughflow current; the onset of the shallow and ward Leeuwin current; the transition from cold-water carbonate factories to tropical factories. We will test if the Bare Fm. correspond to the stratigraphic component of environmental reorganization of the NWS in the Neogene, correlating timing and concentration of siliciclastic deposition with timing of Neogene changes in regional climate and oceanography. Additionally, we expect to observe singular stratigraphic features within the Bare Fm. that are connected to those regional transitions. For example, presence of karstification and sinkhole are associated with subaerial exposure of the shelf during sea-level falls, and has been associated with falling stage system tracts (FSTT; \citep{Raine_2017} .
  • Sedimentary strata deposited during the middle-to-late Miocene in global continental margins are strongly linked, representing the global mid-Miocene siliciclastic influx event.
A similarity between the sedimentary processes that affected different continental margins between the Middle Miocene to Late Miocene would suggest a connection between these events. The proposed global mid-Miocene siliciclastic influx will be verified if the different siliciclastic formations observed in continental margins are equivalent in lithostratigraphy, timing of deposition and sediment volume.
  • The mid-Miocene siliclastic influx is a stratigraphic response to the global Miocene climatic transition.
If biostratigraphic ages and depositional conditions of siliciclastic influxes across continental margins are synchronized, occurring globally in a short period of time, the idea of an allochthones, global cause is reinforced, since it affects all continental margins equally, and at the same time. On the other hand, a larger variability in depositional systems and timing of deposition, may indicate a disconnection between these sedimentary processes, reinforcing the importance of local/regional processes affecting the continental margin stratigraphy.

II. Exp 356 and 369 Transect: Neogene Paleoclimate variation along Western Australia.

IODP Exp 356 exposed the latitudinal control on Neogene deposits of NWS. It observed two distinct group of lithologies, one in sites U1458 and U1459, and the second from U1461 to U1464. It also identified a progressive depositional trend between U1461 and U1464, from arid to wetter depositional conditions (Gallagher et al., 2017; Christensen et al., 2017). Those trends reflect climatic variability along latitudes. In that way, northern sites tend to reflect more tropical characteristics whether southern sites display more arid evidences. Oceanographic divides between oceanic currents are also manifested on a latitudinal transect.
Additionally, it is important to consider the impacts of continental drift and dynamic topography in driving depositional changes during the Neogene of Western Australia. Northward drift of Australia brought NWS into tropical latitudes (Veevers et al., 1991; Muller et al., 2008a), that resulted in a depositional transition from siliciclastic to carbonate deposition on the NWS (Gallagher et al., 2017). Dynamic topography models indicate that the western Australia underwent through an intensification of a tilting processes during the Cenozoic. As the continent moved away from a mantle anomaly, and the Banda Arc subduction zone is established in the north, at least 100 m of vertical space was created at the NWS due to continental tilt, causing an increase in accommodation space and the consequent inundation of the NWS \citep{DiCaprio_2009,DiCaprio_2011}.
Fluctuating siliciclastic content throughout the Neogene carbonate section cored by Expedition 356 has provided crucial information on the evolution of aridity in northwestern Australia and the development of the Australian Monsoon. IODP Exp 369 will allow us to further extend the latitudinal transect, englobing the entire Western Margin (Figure 1). The transect will represent a complete sequence of Neogene sites from temperate, cool-water areas, to tropical, warm-areas.

Data

Sites MBAS-6A, MBAS-8A and MBAS-9A are planned to recover Neogene sediments (Figure 1; Hobbs et al., 2016). Proceeding chapters of IODP 369 will include lithological descriptions, biostratigraphic ages. Logging tools include gamma ray, color reflectance, porosity, density, magnetic susceptibility, uranium, thorium and potassium concentrations.

Hypothesis

Exp 356 and 369 Transect: Neogene Paleoclimate variation along Western Australia.
The extended transect will allow us to further constrain the onset and development of the Leeuwin current and its effects on sedimentation, particularly the boundary between tropical/subtropical and temperate carbonate deposition. Moreover, differential subsidence between the transect limits is expected to be at least 100 m,  have impacted accommodation space generation, and hence, sedimentary deposition, in the margin. Spectral gamma ray logs may prove to be particularly valuable for tracking the siliciclastic contribution, as they have been for Expedition 356 where they may allow the distinction of fluvial from aeolian contributions, thereby providing enhanced links between sedimentation and climate. 
  • What is the lithological heterogeneity along the transect? How siliciclastic varies along the margin? How differential subsidence along the margin affected siliciclastic distribution?
The more tropical conditions in NWS than in SWS possibly intensify run-off activity, and subsequent, siliciclastic transport and deposition. However, it also intensifies carbonate production, and so the siliciclastic signal may not be clear. At the same time, differential subsidence should be less pronounce in SWS, so we expected a more condensed section for Cenozoic sedimentation. Exp 356 results suggest that west Australia became wetter during the late Miocene creating a climate gradient with the arid interior, while northwest Australia remained arid throughout (Groneveld et al., 2017). That should also be reflected in Qz% and K% in SWS.

Cenozoic and Neogene Regional sedimentation trends in Western Australia are strongly linked to the evolution of local oceanic currents (e.g. Leeuwin current and Indonesian Throughflow). The establishment of the Indonesian Gateway and the opening of the Tasman passage forced a reorganization of these currents. We expect to observe, in the stratigraphic record, the signals of these oceanic events. For example, IODP Exp 356 results, based on %K,  suggested that the constriction of the Indonesian Throughflow current during the Late Neogene (5.5 Ma - 3.3 Ma) transferred control of the northwest Australian climate from the Pacific to the Indian Ocean, resulting in an increase of aridity in the margin (Christensen et al., 2017). A diffuse propagation of these effects to the southern margin is expected. current caries along the margin, Furthermore, the transect emcompass virtually the entire area affected by the Leeuwin current. We will be able to address, based on the stratigraphic signals, major changes in the current's characteristics (i.e., change from warm water to cool water deposition).

III. A Coupled Stratigraphic - Landscape Evolution Model of SW Australian Continental Margin: climate-tectonic interactions in the Neogene.

The recent advance in computational techniques allowed the development of new quantitative methods in geosciences. For the first time, it is possible to simulate synthetically the geological processes in its original scales in space and time (e.g. millions of years, hundreds of kilometers). Many modeling techniques in earth surface processes have been developed in the recent time, including notably, the efforts of the Community Surface Dynamics Modeling System (CSDMS), a hub of development and distribution of open-source scientific programs to simulate Earth's processes, with diverse purposes and programming languages. In sedimentary systems, modeling development has been concentrated on landscape evolution models (i.e. erosion and transport processes), with a focus on modern systems and future predictions. Given the relative short scale of modern environments, tectonic and stratigraphic processes are often neglected in landscape evolution models. 
At the same time, stratigraphic studies are limited by the degree of uncertainty of stratigraphic interpretations, that often enables more than one possible outcomes of stratigraphic analysis. For that reason, an attempt to enhance descriptions and further limit the degree of interpretation is currently in place by the scientific community. This is particularly clear in the sequence stratigraphy community, attempting to standardize its concepts and ideas to produce more consistent results \citep{Catuneanu_2009}. Recent advances in modeling of passive continental margins include SEDSIM, SedFlux and Dionisos \cite{Csato_2014,Griffiths_2001,Hutton_2008}. Therefore, stratigraphic studies would greatly benefit by the development and application of numerical models.
In this context, SIGNUM (Simple Integrated Geomorphological Numerical Model) is a Matlab, TIN-based landscape evolution model, that simulates landscape evolution in different scales.  It uses an explicit finite-volume scheme that requires a CFL upper limit for the time step \citep{Refice_2012}. Particularly, it is capable of simulating hillslope processes such as linear and nonlinear diffusion, fluvial incision into bedrock, spatially varying surface uplift which can be used to simulate changes in base level, thrust and faulting, as well as effects of climate changes, using three main equations \citep{Refice_2012}:
  1. Stream Power Equation: \(\frac{∂z}{∂t}=-K_cA^m(∇z)^n\), represents the rate of fluvial incision as a power law function of water discharge and local channel gradient. The drainage area A is a proxy for the water and thus the sediment flux. Kc is a coefficient of erosion that includes erosion process, bedrock erodibility, climate, vegetation, sediment flux \citep{Whipple_1999}. m and n are positive constants, and ∇z is the gradient of elevation. This equation, however, considers that all eroded material exit the surface, and so, a conservative counterpart is necessary to simulate deposition.
  2. Uplift: \(\frac{∂z}{∂t}=u_f\), calculates elevation rate based on a constant uplift; where u_f is a parameter to be assigned in the parameter file.
  3. Linear Diffusion Equation: \(\frac{∂z}{∂t}=-K_d∇^2z\), calculates…. where K_d is the diffusion coefficient.
Additionally, DynEarthSol3D is a long-term tectonic model (>10000 years). It uses a elastoviscoplastic approach (EVP) that allows it to simulate tectonic deformation, lithospheric flexure,  and rheological models for the flow of rocks \citep{Choi_2013}. By solving the momentum balance and heat equation in Lagrangian form, it has been successfully used to model mid-ocean ridge initiation, normal fault evolution, and glacier and ice sheet deformation \citep{Logan_2016}. We propose to integrate DynEarthSol3D tectonic model, with SIGNUM landscape evolution model. Coupling landscape evolution models with tectonic and stratigraphic models constitute an opportunity to simulate the interactions and feedback between local and regional controls to the development of continental margins.

Hypothesis

  • Tilting of Australian Continent after Late Cretaceous, and its effects on the margin evolution.
Dicaprio et al. (2009, 2015) proposed a long and continuous northward tilting of Australian continent after Late Cretaceous, and a consequent inundation of the  northern margin. At least 100 m of gradient in elevation is expected between NWS and SWS, as a result of Cenozoic tilting. A concomitantly gradual and continuous shift of erosion and sedimentation processes towards north, as a response to accommodation space generation, is also anticipated.
  • Feedbacks and interactions of intraplate stress and climate driving landscape evolution.
Neogene record of the West African margin shows a pulse of terrigenous sedimentation during the Miocene, that is also concomitantly with tectonic reactivation of the margin that caused the uplift of  at least 500 m and consequent erosion of the shelf \citep{Lavier_2001}. The same period, globally, is marked by a greenhouse-icehouse transition, with a drastic change in δ18O (‰) values, and sea level fall. Therefore, in that case, both tectonic and climatic processes caused a pulse in terrigenous deposition in the margin (i.e. progradation and shelf erosion), although the increase in sedimentation off Angola seems to be dominated by the effect of global cooling, as it correlates with the middle Miocene d δ18O shift. NWS of Australia present a similar behavior of tectonic reactivation and increase in terrigenous sedimentation during the middle Miocene. Our goal is to constrain the mechanisms driving the increase in terrigenous sedimentation. For example, I will address how tectonic uplift/subsidence/intra-plate stress and climate affect erosion rates and, as a result, the amount of sediment transported to the margin.

PRELIMINARY RESULTS

I. Middle-late Miocene siliciclastic influx on the Australian Northwest Shelf: origins and potential links to global events

Seismically, the Bare Fm. is characterized by a large amplitude, and partially discontinuous set of seismic reflectors immersed in an overall continuous and homogeneous set of seismic reflectors that constitute the carbonates of Trealla Formation (Figure 4). Maximum seismic thickness in two-way travel time (TWT) yields 340 ms, resulting in a thickness of 780 m with a p-wave average velocity for the interval of 2300 m/s (U1462C p-wave velocity data). Bedding is heterogeneous along strike line, with bidirectional bedding along the facies and mounded shape on the depocenter; along dip, bedding is partially homogeneous, following the regional tilting of X degrees. Lateral continuity of horizons within the Bare Fm. is dual along strike, showing both local lateral continuity with the carbonate rocks, and abrupt truncations. Three cycles of onlap/continuous set of seismic reflectors of the Trealla Fm. into the Bare Fm. are recognized (Figure 5). In the northern limits of the Bare Fm., analysis of the dip lines sheds light on the position of the sandstones along the shelf: seismic horizons from both coastal facies and deep-water facies onlap into the mounded-shape facies of the Bare Fm., showing a detached character of these sandstones in that areas. On the southern limit, instead, seismic horizons are continuous both landward and basinward. Three chronostratigraphic intervals are determined based on age control points at U1462C:
Late-Miocene chronostratigraphic interval comprises the deepest portion of the Bare Fm. Along strike, seismic reflectors are mounded-shape and bidirectional (SW-NE). Reflectors terminations are either truncation or downlap, and basal contact is erosional (Figure 4). Along dip, morphology is characterized by two seismic bodies (mound 1 and 2; Figure 6b). Mound 1 is 50 km long, up to 6 km wide, and 50 ms TWT thick (115 m; with 2300 m/s velocity). In the seismic slice, mound 1 is sigmoidal, elongated, with cross-stratification subparallel to SW-NE (Figure 6c). As the mound develops through time, it retrogrades landward, while its front migrates NE. Mound 2 is 26 km long, up to 5 km wide, and 70 ms TWT thick (160 m; with 2300 m/s velocity). Mound 2 is elongated, with oblique cross stratifications in the SW-NE direction (Figure 6d). Messinian - Piacenzian (5.59 – 3.47 Ma) seismic interval marks the thickest package of sedimentation of the Bare Fm. It also marks the full development of mound 3 at the depocenter and the peak of progradation of the Bare Fm. (Figure 6e). Coherence and seismic slices reveal a shift in the depositional patterns from the previous interval. Mound 3 is dip-oriented, with X m in the strike direction, and X m in the dip direction at its maximum size, near the top limit of the interval. Piacenzian – Gelasian (3.47 – 1.93 Ma) seismic interval reveals the final stage of siliciclastic deposition and the establishment of a retrogradational pattern following the peak in progradation at ~3.47 Ma. Mound 3 gradually decreases in size and migrates NE, as sediment supply is reduced. At the top of the interval (~1.93 Ma) retrogradation accelerates, and the seismic facies that characterizes the Bare Fm. vanishes.

  • Depositional System – Deltaic vs. Beach Barrier

Sanchez et al., (2016, 2012b) suggested that siliciclastic deposition of the Bare Fm. occurred as a result of a deltaic progradation over the carbonate-dominated shelf during the Late Miocene, that extended towards the shelf-edge. An alternative interpretation proposed that the absence of fluvial channels recognizable in 3D seismic data, the along-strike orientation of the deposits, and the overall regional geology interpreted for the period would advocate for a beach-barrier system (Smith et al., 2014; O’Callaghan et al., 2014; Griben et al., 2016). Our analysis contributes to untie the present duality in the interpretation of the depositional system.
Paleodepth estimation of IODP U1462 site does not further detail Late Miocene paleobathymetry, due to the lack of representative fossils samples in the period (Gallagher et al., 2017a). However, lithostratigraphy and biostratigraphy of site U1464 recognized near-shore facies for the middle-late Miocene, between 0-50 m paleodepth. The presence of strike-oriented bodies, lateral accretion, karstification, and anhydrite nodules, further support the interpretation of a near-shore environment, under the effect of wave action, shallow-water currents, and eventually exposed.
Additionally, a mixed siliciclastic/carbonate coastal system is interpreted for Unit IV of U1464, in the middle-late Miocene period (12.12 - 5.59 Ma; McHugh et al., 2017, AGU). In a semi-restricted sabkha environment, sandstones occur in the form of sand dunes in the backshore, beach ridges and channel inlets in the nearshore, and sandbars and beach barriers in the shoreface \citep{Warren_1985}. In these systems, karstification and sinkhole activity occur by subaerial exposure of evaporates during sea-level falls, and has been associated with falling stage system tracts (FSTT;  (Raine et al., 2017). We interpret that the basal interval of the Bare Fm. formed in a sabkha depositional system, in the form of beach barriers and/or sand bars. Bare Fm. deposition initiates after the global middle Miocene sea-level fall, following the middle-Miocene climatic optimum, that exposed the shelf, resulting in karstification and the development of the mixed siliciclastic/carbonate system. However, such interpretation for the Messinian - Piacenzian (5.59 – 3.47 Ma) interval is not as clear, as a dip-oriented sedimentary mound appears.
Two main hypotheses may be considered for the upper deposition: a beach spit system within a broader beach-barrier system, concomitantly with sandstone reworking and an increase in sediment supply; or a point-based source, in a form of a fluvial/deltaic environment. Then again, large-scale fluvial channels, channel belts, and deltaic forms are usually recognizable at the scale of the seismic survey, but no indication of fluvial channels and channel belts were found in the seismic analysis. Fluvial channel incisions and channel filling, considering the volume of siliciclastic sedimentation, would also be expected, but none was identified in the seismic area.
Previous studies have pointed to the high degree of similarity between features Gilbert-type deltas and beach spit systems, highlighting the presence of giant-scale crossbedding in both systems \citep{Nielsen_1988}. Wave and longshore processes are also common in wave-dominated deltas \citep{Bhattacharya_2003}, adding more difficulty to correctly interpret these systems. \citep{Evans_1942} described the characteristics and heterogeneity of spits and related structures, and defined a spit as a ridge of sediment attached to the land mass on one end and terminating in open water at the other, being younger than the land mass to which it is attached. Subsequently, \citep{Meistrell_1972} defined spit platform as a large-scale primary sedimentary structure formed by sediment transport along the coast and is a rise over the shelf but below mean low tide. A spit, by that definition, is a sediment ridge on the spit platform. According to the model, initiation of a spit system occurs when oblique waves create an oblique bar-trough system on the platform in the surf zone along the seaward side of the spit, and sand is transported forward to the edge of the platform by trough currents and migration of bars \citep{Nielsen_1988}.\cite{Nielsen_1988}
We consider the depositional system of the Bare Fm. to be analogous to a spit-platform system, within a broader beach-barrier system that developed over the sabkha environment, in three distinct phases (Figure 7):
  • phase 1; formation of mounds 1 and 2, in a sabkha environment transitioning to a beach-barrier system. due to an increase in siliciclastic supply. Wave and longshore action parallel and subparallel to the coast transport and rework siliciclastic sediments, forming strike-oriented sandbars with giant scale cross-bedded set \citep{Nielsen_1988}.
  • phase 2; increase in wave action obliquity (SW-NE), enabling lateral migration along the coast and sandspit initiation. An increase in siliciclastic availability and wave/longshore energy results in rapid expansion of the sandspit, and progradation of the beachline.
  • phase 3; siliciclastic availability considerably reduces, and beachline retrogrades, interrupting sandspit development.

  • Paleoceanography and Paleoclimate Implication

Following the global Miocene Climate Optimum (~17-15 Ma), an increase in ice volume on Antarctica and a drop in global sea level occurred at ~14 Ma. Additional cooling during Late Miocene is interrupted by a subtle warming trend in the early Pliocene (6 Ma) until ~3.2 Ma \citep{Zachos_2001}. Continued sea surface cooling was accompanied by increasingly wet conditions in southwestern Australia reaching a maximum at ~8 Ma (Groeneveld et al., 2017. in review). Arid conditions in northwestern Australia were similar to today’s, and persisted for most of the Miocene, while southwestern Australia became continuously wetter during the late Miocene until ~8 Ma (Groeneveld et al., 2017. in review). From Late Miocene to Pleistocene, as Australia continent moved north, it influenced regional and global climate. Northwest Australia underwent an abrupt transition to humid conditions at 5.5 Ma, lasting until 3.3 Ma, and followed by a transitional interval characterized by a progressive moisture reduction, culminating in an Arid Interval after ~2.4 Ma (Christensen et al., 2017. in review).
Siliciclastic transport to margins is mostly performed by fluvial systems, that in turn, are ultimately controlled by climate conditions (e.g. humidity and run-off;). Given the regional variability of the climate at the time of Bare Fm. development, a linkage between those processes is expected. Increasing cooling and wetting conditions in the Middle Miocene of Western Australia (reaching its maximum at 8 Ma), propitiously engendered siliciclastic transport and deposition to the margin. Internal variation in the depositional system of the Bare Fm. at ~5.59 Ma, and a peak in sedimentation following it (~5.59 – 3.47 Ma) is possibly linked to a regional transition to humid conditions (5.5 - 3.3 Ma). Next, a gradual decrease in sediment supply, and the subsequent termination of siliciclastic deposition may represent the sedimentary response to the emergence of an arid period after ~2.4 Ma.

  • Importance for Global Middle to late Miocene Tectonics and Climate

The Neogene period is marked by dramatic changes in climate and tectonic patterns, that affected sea level and sediment supply rates and, consequently, continental margin evolution \citep{Potter_2009,Potter_2015}. A general trend of increasing terrestrial sedimentation rates is observed throughout the Cenozoic on many different margins (Molnar., 2004), and has been linked to the cooling trend that began during the middle Miocene and intensified in the Pliocene and Quaternary \citep{Zachos_2001}. Potter and Szatmari (2009, 2015) suggest that the increase in sedimentation rates and the alteration in climate patterns developed during the Miocene are result of tectonic processes, that led to an increase in ice sheet development, a reorganization in the global oceanic currents, and a peak in tectonic orogeny elsewhere, modifying global climate and establishing a cooling trend after the mid-Miocene climatic optimum \citep{Zachos_2001}.
A likely interpretation is that ice-sheet development and an increase in aridity resulted in lower sea level, reduced vegetation cover, and increasing in erosion rates. Cooler temperatures also amplified the effects of normally weak Milankovitch cycles, increasing amplitudes of ice-sheet expansion and contraction \citep{Eyles_1996}. At the same time, new orogenic areas also contributed to the increased rates of sediment supply to margins. Those processes are recorded in the stratigraphy on many continental margins. The Bare Fm. constitutes an example of a postulated global pulse of siliciclastic sediments in continental margins during the Middle to Late Miocene Siliciclastic Influx.
